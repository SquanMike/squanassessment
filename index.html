Before you open the door: check both IDs

Every inspector carries a letter of introduction AND a photo ID badge issued by the Manasquan Police Department. Ask to see both.

No badge? No entry. Inspectors will never ask for payment, banking, or personal financial information, and will only enter a home when an adult is present.

Any concerns at all — call the Manasquan Police Department at (732) 223-1000 before letting anyone in.

WHAT TO EXPECT

The inspection takes just a few minutes

Outside

The exterior of every house and structure is measured and photographed.

Inside

The inspector confirms living area and the condition of kitchens, baths, basements & attics.

Not home? No problem

You'll get a card at your door with a number to call to schedule a convenient time. First visits are unannounced; visits happen days, evenings & weekends.

They gather data only

Inspectors do not set your assessment. Values are determined by the Tax Assessor and NJ-certified appraisers.

This is NOT a code inspection

Inspectors are not looking for code, zoning, or permit violations — that is not their job. They collect only the information needed to value your property, and it goes only to the Tax Assessor.

Planning to appeal? Let them in anyway

If inspectors can't get inside, they must record an estimate — and an appeal of an estimated value will require an interior inspection eventually anyway. Letting them in now takes a few minutes and saves you time and hassle later.

Why is my assessment only about half my home's value?

What the ratio is: every year, the NJ Division of Taxation compares the actual sale prices of Manasquan properties that sold against their assessed values. The average of those comparisons becomes the official "average ratio" (the Director's Ratio), certified each October for every town in the State. For 2026, Manasquan's ratio is 50.30% — meaning the typical Manasquan property is assessed at about half of what it would actually sell for, because assessments were last set to market more than a decade ago and prices have roughly doubled since.

HOW TO ESTIMATE WHAT THE TOWN'S MATH SAYS YOUR HOME IS WORTH
Your assessed value  ÷  0.5030  =  implied market value
Example — the Mayor's home: $678,300 ÷ 0.5030 ≈ $1.35 million. That's roughly the value the reassessment will test against actual inspection and sales data.

But 50.30% is only an average. Some properties sit well above it, some well below — which means the tax burden has quietly drifted out of balance between neighbors. The reassessment resets every property to 100% of today's value so everyone's share is measured by the same yardstick.

And no — a doubled assessment does not mean a doubled bill. When the town's total value rises, the tax rate falls correspondingly. The reassessment does not raise one extra dollar for the Borough, the schools, or the County. Whether your individual bill rises, falls, or stays flat depends only on how your value changed relative to everyone else's.

Of every dollar on a Manasquan tax bill, only about 23 cents stays with the Borough to fund local services — police, public works, beaches, and everything else Borough government does. The rest passes through to the school district, Monmouth County, and the fire district.

Yet under New Jersey law, it's the municipality that must run the entire assessment and collection function — including this reassessment — on behalf of all of them. Your Borough is doing this work in-house, with its own Tax Assessor's staff, because the law requires it and because fair, accurate values protect every Manasquan taxpayer.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Quick answers

Who is doing the inspections?
Trained inspectors from the Borough Tax Assessor's own staff — this reassessment is being done in-house, not by an outside firm. Every inspector carries a letter of introduction and a Manasquan Police Department photo ID badge.
Do I have to let them in?
You never have to answer the door at an inconvenient time. But an actual interior inspection is in your interest: if inspectors can't get inside after two visits, they must record a considered estimate of your interior — and if that estimate is wrong, your value could be too. Even if you expect to appeal, an appeal of an estimated value will require an interior inspection eventually anyway — so letting the inspector in now costs you less time, not more. If you miss them, just call the number on the card they leave.
Are inspectors checking for code or zoning violations?
No. This is not a code enforcement, zoning, or permit inspection, and inspectors are not looking for violations. Their only job is to gather the information needed to fairly value your property — living area, condition, and features — and that information goes only to the Tax Assessor for valuation purposes.
Will my taxes go up?
Not necessarily. The reassessment collects the same total levy — it doesn't raise one extra dollar. As values rise to 100% of market, the tax rate falls correspondingly. Your individual bill depends on how your property's value changed relative to everyone else's, and can't be predicted until the reassessment is complete and the new rate is computed.
What affects my home's value?
Total living area in square feet (not a room count), plus condition and features: remodeled kitchens and baths, finished basements, central air, decks, patios, pools, garages, land features, and overall condition. Physical condition also sets depreciation for age and use.
What if I disagree with my new value?
First, request a free informal one-on-one review with the Tax Assessor's office — they'll walk through exactly how your value was reached. Still not satisfied? File a formal appeal with the Monmouth County Tax Board before January 15. Bring recent sales of comparable properties. Remember: you can appeal your assessment, not your taxes.
When does the new value take effect?
The new assessment becomes the basis of your 2027 taxes. It first appears on the bill you receive in July 2027, with the first payment due August 1, 2027.
Why is this happening at all?
The Monmouth County Board of Taxation ordered Manasquan (and several other towns) to reassess. NJ law requires assessment at 100% of true market value, and after 10+ years ours have drifted to about half that — unevenly. The purpose is a fair distribution of the same tax levy.
